        The elephant in the room for all this is that you're trying to do cutting-edge, complex engineering using physics that is STILL poorly understood at a conceptual level.

        For example, a major open question in physics is whether gravity alone can spontaneously "collapse", "decohere", etc. quantum systems. Frankly, that 'collapse' itself is the greatest magical black box in all of science. Nobody knows yet, conceptually, how or why it happens. It is literally bolted on the rest of quantum mechanics as a mysterious, conceptually vague axiom you need to go from the (Alice in) Wonderland of "wavefunctions" and "probability amplitudes" to statistical predictions of measurement outcomes out here in the real world.

        Right now, anybody who tries to build a general purpose quantum computer is also effectively conducting a major, many-body quantum physics experiment. The very regime in which quantum physics has been tested the LEAST, and therefore any new physics due to 'quantum gravity' or other effects is most likely to have observable consequences. This will be great for science, but it could also add all sorts of currently unknown engineering challenges. My guess is that we'll have something useful and general within 10-15 years, but getting up to 1000 qbit or other ultra-powerful systems will take far longer than the comparable jump did for classical computes.

                        In a quantum computer memory and processor are the same. All the data the quantum computer is going to use for a calculation must be mapped to the qubits at the start of the calculation. During the calculation, the quantum computer cannot access external memory. Roughly speaking, if your problem requires a million bits of data, you will need a quantum computer with a million qubits.

                              Peter Morgan Mustapha Ajam 3 years ago

                              We can differentiate classical from quantum computation very much as you suggest: a system state that evolves so that it ensures that all measured signals are either very close to "0" or very close to "1" whenever a clock signal happens (there is nonlinear feedback hardware that ensures this discreteness), and those signal values are determined by what all the signal values were when the previous clock signal happened, is a digital computer, a physical model of a Turing machine. When the determinism fails, which it occasionally will, because of noise, even if there is error correction, that's an error. [Computers may be engineered, for example, to allow different parts to operate asynchronously for the sake of efficiency, but that engineering is intended to ensure equivalence to a Turing machine.]

                              A system state that evolves without measurement at fixed time intervals (so we don't know whether measurements of any given signal is near 0 or 1 or near any other value at intermediate times) is already an analog computer; if there is also noise and we implement incompatible measurements (represented by operators that do not commute), we have a quantum computer.

                                    Ty Rex Vasiliu Mirel 4 years ago edited

                                    Yes, only a particular solution is obtained - but the computation is set up so that it is the one you are looking for.

                                    For example, in the prime factorization algorithm, the possible measurement outcomes are each separated by a large number that solves the particular factorization problem, so just a few repetitions of the algorithm gives this number. Classically, as far as we know, to generate the periodic probability distribution of the outcomes would take a classical computer decades or more (for factorizing large numbers), as they would have to sum up over a large number of possibilities under certain constraints to get the final probabilities. But a quantum computer automatically sums up a whole lot of amplitudes instead (which are like square roots of probabilities): the possibilities are like wavelets, and these automatically add up in the device to give a single wave amplitude at the end. Measuring this wave then "collapses" it, giving the final probability distribution.

                                    Other problems are solved in different ways, but can generally be viewed as exploiting a decomposition into wavelet amplitudes (in many dimensions) in a similar way.
